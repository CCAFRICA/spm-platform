## §1 — PROBLEM STATEMENT

**The validator enforces conformance to anticipated forms, not correctness — and in doing so certifies a defect as valid.** Tonight's MIR plan reimport (production, 2026-06-23) shows PrimeValidator warning on every gate and rate constant across all five plans: *"Constant in compare position lacks meta={unit,scale,confidence}. Convergence may need to infer scale."* The defect chain:

1. The interpreter **expresses the unit correctly.** "80%" is self-describing; the model knows it is a percentage attainment threshold. This is not a capability gap.
2. The prime_dag construction path **strips the unit**, reducing "80%" to a bare scalar (`0.80` or `80`). The meaning that was carried *in* the value now lives nowhere — it must be supplied by an implicit convention the interpreter and convergence silently share ("percentages are stored as decimals").
3. The validator then **flags the correct-but-stripped value as non-conformant**, and the reflexive remedy (annotate the scalar with `{unit,scale}`) launders the stripped value back into conformance. **The validator is not catching a defect; it is validating the defective transformation in, under a PASS.**

The implicit storage convention is a **registry** — developer-maintained, fixed, external to the value, requiring producer and consumer to share it for the comparison to mean anything. It is the ColumnRole-enum / signal_type-registry anti-pattern manifesting on numbers instead of strings, and an *implicit* convention is worse than an explicit enum: nothing fails loud when the two sides disagree; the payout is simply wrong.

**Accuracy impact is categorical, not marginal.** Band selection and gate activation are discontinuous, so a scale mismatch does not produce a near-miss — it selects the wrong branch entirely: on a percentage attainment band, the payout collapses to the wrong band (zero vs. top); on a percentage activation gate, the gate never opens and the whole plan pays zero; on a decimal rate stored as its percentage face value, the result is 100× — silently, with the IAP score potentially passing.

**Interrogating the validator decomposes it into registries.** Its checks are of the form *constant ∈ a known {unit,scale} set*, *op ∈ a recognized set*, *shape ∈ {banded_lookup, arithmetic, conditional}* — each "does this match what I enumerated." A validator-of-anticipated-forms standing in front of a recognizer is architecturally opposed to it: it rejects precisely the novel-but-correct expression the recognizer exists to produce, and can never be complete (Korean Test). The prior architect law — *validation is only as effective as the set of validations it carries* — is the definition of a registry, and therefore the proof that set-membership validation is the wrong category.

**The fix is subtraction plus carry, not annotation.** Stop stripping (persist the value as the model expressed it, unit carried); delete the set-membership checks (do not satisfy them); normalize at the point of comparison from the carried natures, failing loud on incompatibility. This is the structural class, not the MIR instance — every validator is swept (SR-34 / Adjacent-Arm Drift).

**Behavior change is scoped and explicit (DD-7):** this HF deletes set-membership validation and changes construction to carry the unit. It does **not** alter any verification check, any calc-engine read contract, or any already-stored prime_dag. No behavior is expanded beyond the named registry deletion and carry-not-strip change.
